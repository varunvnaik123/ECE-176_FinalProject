The ~20 percentage point confidence drop from synthetic to real data is the core finding. The model still assigns confident predictions on real data (79% mean, well above the 14.3% random baseline), but this confidence is not matched by directional accuracy — see Notebook 05 backtest. The 67:15 bearish:bullish ratio likely reflects both a slight model bias toward bearish pattern shapes and the market conditions during the test period.

4 — Grad-CAM: What Is the Model Looking At?

Grad-CAM computes the gradient of the predicted class score with respect to the last convolutional layer's feature maps. Regions with large gradients are highlighted — these are the parts of the chart the model considered most important for its prediction.

The Grad-CAM heatmaps confirm the model is learning the right things — it attends to the peaks, troughs, and structural shape of each pattern, not random candles or background regions. Head & Shoulders shows strong activation on the three peaks and neckline. This rules out "shortcut learning" and validates the synthetic training approach.

5 — Backtest: Does It Make Money?

For each detected pattern (confidence ≥ 0.55, not neutral), we check what the S&P 500 price did over the next 5 trading days. A bullish signal is "correct" if price went up; bearish if price went down.

Actual results (notebook 05)

MetricResultInterpretation
Directional accuracy — overall 37.3% Significantly below random — model mispredicts direction
  Bullish signals 38.5% (n=13) Below 50% baseline
  Bearish signals 37.1% (n=62) Below 50% baseline
t-test p-value vs 50% 0.0272 (significant) Result IS statistically significant — significantly worse than random

The result is stronger than expected. Not only does the model fail to beat random, it is statistically significantly worse than random (p=0.027, accuracy=37.3%). This means the synthetic patterns are anti-correlated with real market structure — the features the model learned (clean Gaussian peaks and troughs) are present in real data but in the opposite direction relative to future price movement. This is the domain gap in its clearest form.

Note: Windows overlap heavily (step=5, window=60), so signals are autocorrelated and the t-test p-value may be optimistic. The directional result is still clear regardless.

Summary of Findings

#FindingEvidence
1 Both models learn synthetic patterns perfectly 100% val accuracy on synthetic data (PatternCNN 13 epochs, ResNet-18 11 epochs)
2 Significant domain gap exists Mean confidence drops from ~99%+ (synthetic) to 79% (real S&P 500)
3 Model attends to correct pattern features Grad-CAM shows activation on peaks, troughs, and pattern structure — not noise
4 Synthetic training actively mispredicts on real data Directional accuracy 37.3%, p=0.027 — statistically significantly worse than random, not just equivalent
5 Transfer learning (ResNet-18) offers no advantage Both models hit 100% at same speed — synthetic domain is different from ImageNet
Core takeaway: CNNs are capable of learning idealized chart patterns with 100% accuracy on synthetic data. However, the domain gap between mathematically generated patterns and real financial markets is so large that accuracy drops to 37.3% — significantly below random guessing (p=0.027). The model is not just failing to generalize; its predictions are anti-correlated with real price movement, costing −30.6% vs a buy & hold return of +30.8%. This confirms the fundamental challenge in financial ML: a model is only as good as the distribution it was trained on, and idealized synthetic distributions can actively mislead real-world inference.
